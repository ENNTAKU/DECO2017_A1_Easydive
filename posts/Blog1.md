This post interprets the EASY DIVE brief through the revised website outline and identifies the main community needs that the prototype is expected to address.

A useful starting point for EASY DIVE is to clarify what kind of value it offers that would not already be available through a general social platform. The brief does not ask for a full social media system. It asks for a focused community experience. In this case, the community is Australian diving enthusiasts, and the revised outline describes a lightweight platform where users can record dives, compare real conditions, browse other people’s logs, and find local dive shop contacts.

This already suggests that the platform should prioritise practical information over broad social interaction. The five-page structure — Home, Log Dive, Explore Sites, Dive Shops, and Profile — makes that direction more explicit. Home introduces the platform and directs users toward the two main actions. Log Dive collects structured records. Explore Sites makes those records searchable. Dive Shops provides local support information. Profile adds lightweight identity and contact context. This is not the structure of a general discussion forum. It is closer to a record-and-browse system shaped around diving activity.

The Log Dive page shows this most clearly. Its fields are specific: state or territory, dive site, site type, date, current strength, visibility, water temperature, surge, marine life, notes, and image placeholder. These fields convert individual experience into information that can later be filtered and compared. The Explore Sites page then depends on the same structure through searching by location, site type, difficulty, and marine life. This means one of the central community needs is not simply communication, but access to shared, localised, experience-based information in a format that is easy to scan.

The Dive Shops and Profile pages extend that model rather than replacing it. Dive Shops connects users to local services through region-based contact cards. Profile introduces certification, experience level, dive count, interest tags, and a contact link. These functions add context and support, but they do not define the platform by themselves. The main value still sits in how user-submitted logs become browseable community knowledge.

The wording on the Home page also reinforces this direction. It frames the prototype as simple and beginner-friendly, with emphasis on current strength, visibility, water temperature, species tags, personal notes, local contacts, and community dive logs. That design choice keeps the platform focused. It avoids trying to simulate professional safety systems or advanced data services, and instead concentrates on what community members can realistically contribute and use.

At this stage, the most accurate reading of the brief is that EASY DIVE should function as a lightweight platform for recording and exploring dive-site experiences rather than as a broad social network.
