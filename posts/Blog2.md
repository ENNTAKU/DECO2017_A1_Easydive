This week, I started thinking less about generating more ideas and more about how the ideas we already have could fit together. At this point, I do not think the problem is choosing one feature and throwing the others away. The harder part is figuring out what the platform should really revolve around.
After more discussion with my teammate, we came up with a clearer list of possible functions for our diving community platform. Some of these came more from his diving experience, while I was thinking more about how they might work as a platform. The features we discussed included choosing dive sites, recording current strength, visibility, marine life, personal reflections, and uploaded images. We also talked about matching dive buddies, online communication, searching for dive-site information, tracking how often certain species appear, and showing local dive shop information and contact details.
When I first looked at this list, my reaction was honestly that everything sounded useful. That made it harder, not easier, to decide what to focus on. At first I thought maybe the solution was to reduce the list as much as possible, but the more I thought about it, the more I realised that this is probably not the right way to look at it. I do not think these features are competing with each other in a simple way. A lot of them actually make more sense together. For example, if users can log dive-site conditions and marine life, that could also make site search more useful later. If people are already recording their experiences, then matching with other divers could feel more grounded as well.
Because of that, I started thinking less in terms of “main feature versus rejected features” and more in terms of core features and supporting features. That feels more realistic for this project. At the moment, I think the strongest core is the dive-site recording side of the platform: things like logging visibility, current strength, marine life, photos, and personal notes. The reason I keep coming back to this is that it feels like the part that can hold the rest together. It is closely tied to the actual diving experience, and it can also generate useful information that other features build on.
For example, site search becomes more meaningful if it is supported by real user records. Species frequency becomes more interesting if it comes from repeated logs. Even local dive shop information feels more relevant if it is connected to real places people are actually diving at. In that sense, I am not seeing the other features as less important. I am starting to see them more as layers around a central activity.
I also think this way of organising the platform makes more sense for development. If we treat every idea as equally central, the whole project could become messy very quickly. But if we decide what the main interaction is first, then the other parts can be designed to support it instead of pulling the platform in too many directions at once.
Right now, my main takeaway is that this stage is less about cutting ideas and more about understanding their relationship. For me, the most promising direction so far is to treat dive-site recording as the centre of the experience, while letting social and informational features grow around it. That feels more focused, and also more realistic for a prototype.
