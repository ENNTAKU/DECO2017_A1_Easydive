This week, I started thinking less about the feature list itself and more about what it would actually feel like to move through the platform as a user. Once I began breaking the platform into pages and actions, I realised that some of the harder design questions are not about what features we include, but about how users arrive at them, understand them, and move between them.
The parts I focused on this week were the ones I am more directly responsible for: user profile information, communication entry points, searching dive-site records, and browsing local dive shop information. Compared to some of the more diving-specific features my teammate is exploring, these parts seem simpler at first. But once I started thinking about user flow and wireframes, I realised they are actually important for making the whole platform feel usable.
The first thing I kept coming back to was the profile page. I do not want it to feel like a generic social media profile, because that would make the platform feel too broad. At the same time, I also do not want it to become too detailed or intimidating. My current thought is that the profile should help users quickly understand who someone is in a diving context without asking them to fill in too much. Things like how long they have been diving, gender if they choose to share it, and diving-related tags seem useful because they make someone easier to understand at a glance. When I imagine the wireframe, I keep feeling that this page needs to stay visually simple. If the profile becomes too crowded, then even the useful information will feel harder to read.
I had a similar reaction when thinking about communication. At first, it seemed easy to just say that users should be able to message each other, but once I thought about the flow, that answer felt too loose. I think communication makes more sense as something users reach through another action, like viewing a profile or finding someone through their dive records, rather than as a separate isolated page. In other words, I do not think people will open the platform just to “go to messaging.” They will probably discover someone first, then want a simple way to contact them. That changed how I thought about the interface, because it made me focus more on where communication starts rather than where messages are stored.
The same kind of shift happened when I thought about searching dive-site information. My first instinct was to imagine a normal search bar and a list of results, but that quickly felt too flat. What users are really looking for is not just a site name. They are looking for other people’s records, observations, and experiences. That made me think the search results need to feel more like browsable record cards than a plain results page. I want the interaction to feel like users are exploring a place through community knowledge, not just retrieving text.
For local dive shop information, I found myself thinking a lot about reducing confusion. If too many shops appear at once, the page could become noisy very quickly. Right now I am leaning towards making users choose a region first, then showing dive shop cards with key information like name, photo, address, and contact details. That feels more manageable both visually and interactively.
What I am realising from this stage is that wireframing is not just about arranging boxes on a page. For me, it is becoming a way to test whether the platform actually makes sense from the user’s side. A feature can sound useful when written as a list item, but once I imagine the clicks, transitions, and page structure, it becomes much easier to see what feels clear and what still feels awkward.
